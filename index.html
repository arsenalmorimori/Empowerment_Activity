      The history of computers dates back to the 1800s, but modern development began in the 1930s with Konrad Zuse's Z1. This led to advancements like Colossus, ENIAC, and IBM's first computer, the IBM 701, in the 1950s. Apple later entered the market with the Apple I, Apple II, and Macintosh. 1970's

// CLICKABLE IMAGE!

The history of modern computing began with Intel's 4004 Microprocessor in 1971, revolutionizing processors with its compact design. Intel continued innovating with the 8-bit 8008 and the x86-based 8086, shaping future computing. Meanwhile, Dennis Ritchie developed the C programming language at Bell Labs, enabling system-level programming and leading to the creation of UNIX, a flexible multi-user OS that inspired later systems like BSD. In 1977, Steve Wozniak's Apple II brought personal computing to the mainstream with affordable hardware, color graphics, and mass-market appeal, powered by the low-cost MOS 6502 processor. This era set the stage for modern computing, influencing hardware, software, and operating systems that continue to shape technology today.

- Jason Robert Carey Patterson

In 1999, U.S. Vice President Al Gore was misquoted as claiming he "invented the internet," though he actually played a key role in its expansion through legislation. Later, in 1989, Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web, making online access more user-friendly. While often confused, the internet is a global network of computers, whereas the Web is the system we use to browse information.
